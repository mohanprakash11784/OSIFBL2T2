Rabindranath Tagore

1861 - 1964

The Bard of Bengal

“At the end of the journey, I am able to see, a little more clearly, the orb of my life. Looking back, the only thing of which I feel certain is that I am a poet.”

A The Bard of Bengal is a sobriquet for Rabindranath Tagore, a Bengali polymath who reshaped Bengali literature and music as well as Indian art with Contextual Modernism in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Author of the "profoundly sensitive, fresh and beautiful" poetry of Gitanjali, he became in 1913 the first non-European and the first lyricist to win the Nobel Prize in Literature.
Tagore's poetic songs were viewed as spiritual and mercurial; however, his "elegant prose and magical poetry" remain largely unknown outside Bengal.
He was a fellow of the Royal Asiatic Society. Tagore was born into a wealthy Bengali family in Calcutta, British India. He was educated at home by private tutors and at the Oriental Seminary.
He began writing poetry at a young age, and his first collection of poems was published in 1878.
Tagore went on to write over 2,000 songs, 1,500 poems, 30 plays, and dozens of novels, short stories, and essays. He also founded an experimental school in rural West Bengal called Shantiniketan, which later became Visva-Bharati University. Tagore's work was influenced by a wide range of sources, including Indian philosophy, Western literature, and the natural world. His poetry is often lyrical and introspective, and it explores themes of love, loss, nature, and spirituality.
Tagore's songs, known as "rabindra sangeet," are popular throughout India and Bangladesh. They are often sung at weddings, festivals, and other social gatherings. Tagore's work has had a profound impact on Bengali culture and society. He is considered to be one of the most important figures in Bengali literature, and his work has been translated into over 50 languages. Tagore's Nobel Prize in Literature was a watershed moment for Indian culture, and it helped to raise the profile of Indian literature and culture around the world. Tagore was a complex and multifaceted figure. He was a poet, playwright, novelist, composer, painter, social reformer, and philosopher. His work was deeply rooted in Indian tradition, but it also spoke to a universal audience. Tagore's legacy is immense, and he continues to be a source of inspiration for people around the world.
